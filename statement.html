I’m interested in how a puppet can hold a sense of “life” — through movement, light, and a subtle response to its surroundings. It’s not robotics in the functional sense; the focus is on aesthetics, presence, and the moment a character feels alive. When I operate a puppet, it’s alive in my hands. Electrical systems just let that life linger when I step away. I’m drawn to settings that carry both pain and hope, where human culture reflects itself in the mechanical, and the mechanical returns that humanity in its own way. I like the meeting point between the warmth of hand-built imperfection and the quiet precision of wires and circuits — where a human, code, electricity, and imagination meet to tell a story through the material itself.

Puppetronics is the meeting point between mechanism and character: how a joint, a string, or a counterweight becomes emotion in space.
